The Star Schema is the most widely used architectural model for data warehouses and data marts. It gets its name from its visual layout: a large, central "Fact" table surrounded by multiple "Dimension" tables, resembling a star.

In this model, the database is purposely denormalized to make it easier for business users to query and faster for the system to generate reports.

1. The Core Components
A star schema consists of two distinct types of tables that serve different purposes:

A. The Fact Table (The "How Much")
Located at the center of the star, the fact table records the quantitative, measurable data of a business process (e.g., a sale, a login, or a temperature reading).

Keys: It contains "Foreign Keys" that link to the surrounding dimension tables.

Measures: It stores numerical metrics like Quantity_Sold, Total_Price, or Profit_Margin.

Growth: This table is typically very "tall"—it can grow to millions or billions of rows as new transactions occur.

B. Dimension Tables (The "Who, What, Where, When")
These tables radiate out from the center and provide descriptive context for the numbers in the fact table.

Attributes: They contain text-based data like Product_Name, Store_City, Customer_Segment, or Month_Name.

Primary Keys: Each dimension table has a unique ID (Primary Key) that connects to the Fact table.

Normalization: Unlike standard databases, these are denormalized, meaning descriptive data is flattened into one table to avoid complex joins.
